ABC Toy Dolly
New Media 203: Critical Making
Professor: Eric Paulos
Team: Mimi Shalf, Lili Dai, Arnold Yeung, Yani Mai, Steffan Cross

I took Critical Making in the spring of 2019 and it was one of the most rewarding classes I took in college. Every single class felt worthwhile and engaging, rare. The course was centered around design, art, and technology with 3 large group projects and a series of smaller individual ones.

One of those larger group projects had a simple focus, movement. Our group wanted to take a lighter approach and design a kids toy. Our initial inspiration came from the dollies that Arnold and I played with in elementary school PE class. Thinking back to those times we thought of the ways we used to play with those dollies, their shortcomings, and possibilities to make them more fun. We also consulted with one of our mentors, who was previously a toy designer and also knew these dollies well as his daughter played with them frequently in school.

Through our memories and consultations, we found that the dollies were only designed to be used as a single object. But, especially in school settings, kids want to and do play in groups. To get around that sometimes they’ll connect the dollies with ropes or hang onto each other. We saw a lot of potential to adapt and make a toy designed for and encouraging of group play.

Abstract
ABC Toy Dolly is a modular toy dolly based on children’s toy dollies and furniture dollies that encourages creativity and collaboration in group games. With embedded sensors, LED Lights, magnetic connectors and an accompanying app, ABC Toy Dolly can provide a wide range of games and help reimagine these classic toys.

Alongside being used as they always have been, multiple games and configurations can be chosen through the app. Some games we planned for the dollies were Blob Tag, Space Invader, and Freeze Tag. With Blob Tag, for example, the LEDs on the tagger’s board would be red while all others would be blue. When the tagger gets within close range of another board the hall effect sensors trigger and the board’s LEDs switch from blue to red. Then those two boards link together and the game continues, compelling the kids to think of unique ways to link their boards and use teamwork to catch their targets.
