isoDome
Architecture 259: Bending and Folding Structures
Professor: Simon Schleicher
Team: Eric Del'Orco, Tina Piracci, Benjamin Taube, Steffan Cross

In this class, our project prompt was to make something that utilized bending or folding in the design. Our group, being all architecture students who are used to making objects at building scale, instead wanted to make something at the product scale.

As one of us had just taken a seminar on building acoustics, we thought that would be an interesting route to take in combination with a folding structure. And, having all had experiences working in open floor plan offices we thought the biggest shortcoming of them is sound privacy.

While open plans are great in that there is hardly any construction required and the arrangement of furniture can be fluid. Constantly being able to hear what others are saying or having background chatter while on calls is unprofessional and harms focus. When researching the issue we found that of all the dissatisfactions in the office environment, sound privacy is by far the number one.

Because of this, we settled on designing some sort of deployable structure that when folded, took up little to no room, but when unfolded provided an acoustically private environment from exterior sounds. We first started by looking at precedent work and also different scales to target, ultimately deciding to make a foldable dome that could house up to six people for meetings.

And, because we didn’t want to just send noise back into the office space we decided absorption would be the best route to go. Gratefully, the human range of speech isn’t very large so we were able to settle on materials and layering that would absorb it sufficiently.

We decided on using a thick acoustic felt and very dense acoustic foam due to their cost and weight. The acoustic foam would be cut to panel size and then sandwiched between sheets of the felt. The felt serves two purposes, it absorbs some sound and is pliable enough to work as a hinge. Because of this, we didn’t need hinges on every panel while at the same time are able to have a completely sealed container such that no noise could leak through small cracks.

Form finding took the most of our time as making a dome collapsible was much harder than any of us had imagined. One of our early prototypes collapsed to be quite compact but the deployment wouldn’t drop down from the ceiling like we’d imagined.

During the course, we worked in collaboration with students from the University of Tokyo and Tomohiro Tachi who made Freeform Origami, the software we used frequently alongside Grasshopper. With his help, the help of our professor and a few models and iterations, we were able to settle on a form. After presenting at the bi-annual Jacobs Showcase on campus, we later found out our project got featured on their site
