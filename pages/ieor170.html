Can One
Industrial Engineering 170: Industrial Design and Human Factors
Professor: Carisa Harris Adamson
Professor: Viraj Attre, Elizabebth Zhou, Ali Mukaled, Steffan Cross

During the spring of 2019, I took Industrial Engineering 170: Industrial Design and Human Factors. Instead of being a design-centric course like I had been accustomed to, the course was more research-centric. It was focused on understanding the human form and how to consider it properly when designing for it. This ended up being one of the most practical courses I took during my time at Berkeley and taught me about the multiple levels one needs to consider when designing products for people.

Our final project was a redesign of an everyday object but with a focus on ergonomics and interaction. The object chosen for my group was a watering can.

At first, we were a little perplexed with what we could do to change the watering can and what was even wrong with it in the first place. However, as we researched more, we found that it was an object with quite a few problems and of all the redesigns done to it, only a small minority made an attempt to remedy its issues.

We found the Union Products 2-Gallon Watering Can to be one of the most popular cans and used it as a standard to compare against. 2 gallons of water translates to 16.7lbs and when considering that of the hobby gardener population, the average age is 50 years old and 60% are female, 16.7lbs isn’t a trivial weight. Further, the conventional design requires it to be placed on the ground when filling, so, again for that population, bending over and picking up that can is another burden along with carrying it from the from where it was filled to the plants. During our interviews, we found that these actions are compounded as the average person refills the can 1-2 times per session.

However, the biggest issue by far was the wrist flexion under tension while pouring. As we found results on the internet and through our interviews, that wrist motion with weight compounded over thousands of pours, can lead to serious injuries. But even long before those added up, many felt discomfort or fatigue in their wrists after watering.

With these issues in mind, we designed a can that strayed far from the form of a conventional watering can. It has a long spout, two large wheels, and a cross grip. We did away with the bending and carrying, instead, one holds the grip near the spout and pulls the can behind from destination to destination. When it comes time to water, without bending over to lift, the can is pulled up from a standing position with both hands. Then, one hand is transferred to the cross grip and the other to the grip along the body. To pour the cross grip is used to support the weight while the can rotates on its wheel axis, driven by an up and down motion and eliminating the wrist strain that had been the main culprit of injuries in the conventional design.
